Introduction

What is Accounting?

Accounting is the recording of transactions in terms of money and the interpretation of results.

Normally this is done for a business, but every individual can also record their own transactions.

The aim of accounting

In Accounting we learn how to record money values of transactions in the books of a business so that the owner have a permanent record of them. From these records we can determine whether the business has made a profit or suffered a loss, and what the financial position of the business is. The aim of Accounting is therefore to keep a record of everything that has happens in business.

Who has an interest in this information?

The owner

  • To know what is happening in the business and to exercise control over it.
  • To determine who owes the business money or whom the business owes money.
  • To calculate the profit or loss.
  • To able to plan ahead.
  • To prevent dishonesty.

Other parties

  • People or other businesses that would like to invest in the business
  • Should the business needs to borrow money from a bank.

Tax

  • Some businesses are registered for VAT and have to pay it.
  • The owner will have to pay income tax.

Assets

Assets can be described as the possessions owned by a business such as money, buildings, vehicles, furniture, equipment, etc. In Accounting we distinguish between two types of assets:

  1. Non-current assets
    • Fixed assets/tangible assets

      • Fixed assets are bought for the purpose of being use4d and not with a view to resell them. These assets, therefore, have a relatively long life. A fixed asse may, however, be sold again shold it no longer be fit for the purpose for which it was purchased:

      1. Land and buldings
      2. Furniture and equipment
      3. vehicles
    • Financial assets

      • Finacial assets include fixed deposits that are invested for longer than 12 months. Should a fixed deposit mature withing 12 months, it is not classified as a non-current asset, but as a current asset.

  2. Current assets

    3. Current assets consists of cash or assets that will be converted into cash withing a relatively short perion, usually 12 months. It also includes amonts owed by debtors these are also possessions of the business, but in a more abstract form:

    1. Money in the bank
    2. Trading stock
    3. Debtors
    4. Fixed deposits that matures within the next 12 months

Owner's equity

This is the interest a person(owner) has in the business. The owner's equity can, also be seen as the amount which the business owes the owner. When the owner starts a business the owner's equity equals the amount that the owner invests in the business that was used to buy assets and pay expenses. Later when a profit is made, the profit will increase the owner's equity, because the business also owes the profit to the owner.

Liabilities

Liabilities are all amounts owed by the business to other persons. Liabilities can therefore be described as all financial contributions made to the business by other persons or businesses and which have to be paid back. We distinguish between two types of liabities:

  1. Non-current liabities

    2. This refers to borrowed money that need not be paid back within the next 12 months.

  2. Current liabities

    3. This refers to debt that has to be paid back within the next 12 months:

    1. Creditors
    2. Bank overdraft
    3. Current portion of loan that has to be paid back within the next 12 months

Expenses

Current operating expenses are all expences that have to be paid so that the business can function. Examples of such are: water and electricity, wage and salaries, telephone expenses, licenses, insurance, etc. Expenses are something that has to be paid, but for which in return bo assets can be shown. These expenses leave the owner poorer and therefore the owner's equity decreases.

Income

When a business receives an income, it means that money is received without another asset being exchanged. In the event of stock sold, the stock is sold for more than it was paid for. An operating income enriches the owner and therefore increases the owner's equity.

The recording of transactions from source documents

Trial balance

Each transations in Accounting has to reflected by two entries in the ledger. This accounting rule reflects the princible of double entry and is the basic principle that has to be strictly adhered to for each transaction made. If this rules is applied throughout, the total of all the debit entries will be equal to the total of the credit entries.

Naturally they have to be the same all the time. This is done by drawing up a trial balance. A trial balance can be described as a list of all final debit and credit balances and the totals of other accounts in the ledger.

Opening of accounts with given balance

In practice a business is only started once, with the main of continuing fro months to months. In practice an account is therefore opened only once and used until the page is full. As soon as the page is full, the account is balanced and balance is carried forward to new page.

    3. As the name indicates, this Subsidiary journal is used for recording all the payments made by the business. The cash payments journal is therefore similar to the credit side of the bank account.
